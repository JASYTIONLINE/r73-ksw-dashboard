Week 06: Iterating on Your Work
Iterating on Your Work
You shipped P01. Nice. Now you need to step back and look at it critically. Avoid the impulse to tear down your work, and focus your time and attention on understanding what's working, what isn't working, and what could and should be improved.

Evaluating Your Own Work
Start by reviewing your deployed project as if you've never seen it before (which can be difficult, but try doing this nonetheless):

Visit your live URL. Don't open it in your editor. Simply visit the deployed site in a browser. Experience it the way your audience would.

Interact with it. Navigate through every page. Click every link. Try every feature. Fill out any forms. Interactive features? Yep: interact with those features. Use it on your phone. Use it on your tablet. Take notes on everything that feels smooth or rough, functions or doesn't, and everything else in between.

Compare it to your proposal. Pull up your A01 project proposal and review it. How does your deployed project compare to what you planned? What did you deliver? What's missing? What's changed? Were the changes intentional or accidental?

Ask yourself some specific questions:

Does the project clearly communicate its purpose within the first few seconds? (Would a random person without context understand this project?)
Is the navigation intuitive? Can a visitor find what they're looking for? (Would a random person be capable of navigating through and finding things?)
Does the interactive feature from Week 04 add genuine value? (Would a random person interact with the interactive feature and find it helpful/fun/worthwhile?)
Is the design consistent across pages? (Would a random person visiting the pages recognize they are related and consistent?)
Does anything feel unfinished, broken, or confusing? (Would a random person encounter pitfalls, confusing areas, broken sections, or partial content?)
Write your answers down. aYou'll use them in your iteration plan (Assignment 02).

Turning Your Evaluation into Action
Start with your own evaluation: the self-assessment you did in the previous section. Peer feedback will arrive in Week 07 through the peer review discussion, and you'll use it for potential final refinements before P02. For now, your own critical eye is your best resource.

Separate observations from opinions. "The navigation doesn't work on a tablet" is an observation describing a specific problem. "I don't like the colors because they are ugly" is an opinion. Both can be useful! However, observations describing specific problems should be addressed first. Opinions are just that: opinions. You might or might not choose to address opinions.

Prioritize by impact. Not all potential improvements are equal. Full stop: prioritizing potential improvements is absolutely critical for anyone building, developing, and designing projects and products. Broken features typically affect usability more than a font choice. Missing pages are a bigger issue than missing animations. This is not necessarily the most exciting work, but it is incredibly impactful. Address what matters most to your project's functionality and purpose first. 

Distinguish between fixing and adding. Some improvements fix problems (broken links, missing content, layout issues. Some improvements add new capabilities (new features, new pages, new interactivity). Fixes should almost always come before additions. Establish a solid foundation for your project with fewer features before adding more scope, features, and complexity. Often, you never actually need or want to add more project scope or features!

Deciding What to Change
You have several weeks left to build before P02 is due. However, that's still not much time. You need to decide which changes will have the most impact on your project's success.

What's broken? Fix it. Broken functionality is the top priority.

What's missing? If your project is missing something essential to its purpose, add it. If it's a nice-to-have, evaluate whether you have time for it.

What could be better? Improvements to existing features (better design, smoother interactions, clearer content) often have more impact than entirely new features.

What should you cut? If you planned a feature in your proposal but now realize it doesn't serve the project, or you don't have time, cut it. Cutting scope is often the legitimately best decision you can make. Document why you cut it in your iteration plan. (Important to note: you cannot say, "I cut my entire project! Winning!")

Key Takeaways
Evaluate your deployed project critically; visit it as your audience would
Compare the deployed project to your original proposal
Prioritize fixes over additions; impact over quantity
Be willing to cut scope if it serves the project
Document your evaluation and decisions in your iteration plan

Week 06: Adding Depth to Your Project
Adding Depth to Your Project
P01 established the foundation for your overall project. Now you must add depth to make your project more functional, more polished, and more complete. A strong foundation was needed to build on, and you did that, so you can now move forward with the project by adding depth to meet your stated goals.

Types of Enhancements
These improvements can fall into a few categories. Most projects benefit from a mix:

Functional Enhancements
Adding capabilities that make your project do more:

A new interactive feature (search, filtering, sorting, dynamic content)
New pages and sections that serve your audience
A form that collects user input and does something useful with it
Integration with an external service or API
Improved navigation or user flow
Visual and Design Enhancements
Making your project look and feel more polished and usable:

Consistent typography, spacing, and color usage across all pages and components
Better responsive behavior on different screen sizes
Smooth transitions and animations that serve a purpose
Improved visual hierarchy to clearly communicate what's most important on each page
Accessibility improvements (sufficient color contrast, keyboard navigation, alt text)
Content Enhancements
Making your content more complete and effective:

Replacing any/all remaining placeholder content
Adding more detail to thin sections
Improving writing clarity and accuracy
Adding images, media, or other visual content
Better organizing content for your audience's needs (note: review your evaluation questions and answers)
Technical Enhancements
Improving how your project works under the hood:

Faster loading times (optimized images, efficient code)
Better deployment configuration
Cleaner file organization
More reliable version control practices
Choosing What to Build
You probably can't do everything in only three weeks. Choose enhancements that:

Serve your project's purpose. Does this addition help your project do what it's supposed to do? Does it serve your audience? What about the other additions and enhancements you listed?

You also need to be realistic about scope. Be realistic! This might be difficult for you, especially since it's your project and you know how great it will be with just a few more enhancements. Can you implement this in the time remaining? If it's a stretch, is there a simpler version that still adds value? Perhaps a simpler version would actually resonate more? Perhaps a simpler version would be more usable?

Make the improvement visible. Internal-only changes (such as cleaner code, better file organization, etc.) matter, but they should be paired with changes others can actually engage with and consider.

And, of course, build on what works. Why tear down something that's working well only to rebuild it differently (or the same)? Refine what you have. 

The Enhancement Process
Adding a new feature or improvement follows the same cycle you used in Week 04:

Describe it. Write a clear description of what you're adding and how it integrates with your existing project.
Implement it. Use your tools to build it. Work incrementally. Avoid trying to change everything at once.
Test it. Verify it works, doesn't break existing features, and functions on mobile, tablet, etc.
Deploy it. Push to GitHub and confirm it works on your live site.
Commit frequently. Each fix and enhancement should be a separate commit with a clear message. Aim for small and meaningful commits. This protects your progress and makes it easier to revert if something breaks.
Key Takeaways
Adding depth means functional, visual, content, and technical improvements to your project
Choose enhancements that serve your project's purpose and are achievable in the remaining time
Build on what works
Work incrementally: describe, implement, test, deploy, commit

Assignment 02: Iteration Plan
Overview
Step back and evaluate what you built after shipping P01. This assignment asks you to honestly assess your P01 prototype, identify what needs to change, and plan your path from P01 to your final MVP in Project 02.

Your iteration plan is the bridge between your first project milestone and your finished project: what to fix, what to add, what to cut, and in what order.

Points: 40

Due: Sunday, April 26 by 11:59 PM CT

Instructions
Submit your iteration plan as a PDF or Markdown file. Your plan should address each of the following four sections.

1. P01 Evaluation (10 points)
Look at your Project 01 with fresh eyes and evaluate it honestly. Address the following:

What works well? Identify the parts of your P01 that are solid: features that function as intended, design choices that landed, and content that communicates clearly, and anything else relevant.
What doesn't work? Identify what fell short: broken features, rough edges, missing content, confusing navigation, or anything that does not meet the standard you want.
How does it compare to your proposal? Look back at Assignment 01: Did you build what you planned? What changed along the way, and why?
Be specific. Be honest. "The navigation is confusing because the menu labels don't match the page content" is useful. "My project needs work, but doesn't work" is not useful. So, again, be specific, be honest, and evaluate with useful, descriptive language.

2. Changes for P02 (15 points)
Based on your evaluation, list the changes you plan to make for Project 02. Organize them into four categories:

Fixes: Things that are broken or not working correctly that must be fixed.
Improvements: Things that work but could be better (e.g., cleaner design, clearer content, smoother interactions, improved layouts).
Additions: New features or content that were not in P01 but belong in your final project.
Cuts: Features or content you originally planned but now realize should be removed. Cutting scope is a legitimate decision, so explain the what and why of any planned cuts.
For each change, write one to two sentences describing what it is and why it matters. 

3. Priority and Timeline (10 points)
Organize your planned changes into three (3) priority tiers:

Must complete: Changes that are required for your project to function as a finished project. These are the non-negotiables.
Should complete: Changes that would meaningfully improve your project but are not strictly required. You plan to do these, but could ship without them if absolutely necessary.
If time allows: Your project's stretch goals. Nice to have, but you will not lose sleep if they do not make it in.
For each tier, include a rough estimate of the potential effort involved. These estimates do not need to be precise: "a few hours" or "most of a weekend" is perfectly fine. The goal is to demonstrate that you have thought about whether your plan is realistic and have spent at least some meaningful time planning what is ahead of you.

4. Updated Tools and Approach (5 points)
Reflect on the tools and approach you used for P01. Address the following:

Are you changing any tools or technologies for P02? If so, what and why? Be specific here, but avoid statements like, "I used some HTML and CSS, but I need to use more HTML and CSS." That type of statement effectively says nothing. Instead, you could say, "My initial work involved plain HTML and CSS files, but I plan to switch to using Astro for site generation. Therefore, I will primarily use scoped CSS for components, with some global, unscoped CSS." 
What tools or workflows were most effective during P01? What will you continue using? Again, be specific here. No statements like, "VS Code is good, and I like typing into it." While it's great that you enjoy a tool, provide more information and context; e.g., "VS Code worked well initially, but after installing a few extensions, getting started with GitHub Copilot for iterative work, and using the built-in Git source control panel, I settled into a workflow that really fit how I enjoy working."
Is there anything you would do differently from the start if you were starting over? Obviously, you do not need to start over, but this reflection matters for the personal and professional projects you undertake in the future.
Submission
Submit your iteration plan as a PDF or Markdown file. Plans evolve. This iteration plan document captures your thinking at this point in time. You are not locked into every single detail, but you should have a clear direction.
