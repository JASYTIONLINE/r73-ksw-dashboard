Week 08: Shipping Your Final MVP
Shipping Your Final MVP
MVP stands for Minimum Viable Product. An MVP is a version of your project that does what it needs to do and includes everything that matters (importantly, however, an MVP does not include everything you could imagine). An MVP works, is deployed, and is documented. You put an MVP into the world.

P02 is your final MVP. Here's what that means and how to make sure you're ready.

What Makes P02 Different from P01
 

P01 was a milestone for your project in its first working state. P02 is the final version.

The differences between P01 and P02 should be visible and meaningful:

Improvements from your iteration plan: You identified fixes, improvements, and additions in A02. P02 should reflect the work you committed to addressing. Not everything needs to be done, but the essential items should be complete! Recall that you prioritized with "must," "should," and "if time allows" tiers.

Peer review feedback addressed (if applicable): If you participated, your classmates should have reviewed your P01 in Discussion 03. If their well-intentioned feedback identified real issues, P02 should show that you considered and addressed them.

Note: most peer feedback is well-intentioned, meaning that there might have been feedback you received that was not applicable, or that you identified as out of scope, etc.
Documentation is complete: P01 had a basic README. P02 has an expanded README and a REFLECTION.md. Your documentation tells the full story of your project.

Polish and completeness: P02 should feel finished from the standpoint that everything present works well and contributes to the project's purpose.

What "Minimum Viable" Means
 

An MVP is usually the smallest version of a product that delivers real value, does what it needs to do, and includes everything that matters. For your project:

It works: Every feature functions as intended. No broken links, no errors, no features that almost work.

It serves its audience: Someone in your target audience can visit your project and get value from it (information, a tool, an experience, a portfolio of your work).

It's deployed and accessible: A URL that anyone can visit (No, you cannot expect anyone to open the local files on your computer)

It's documented: Someone looking at your repository can understand what your project is, why it exists, and how it evolved.

 

Final Submission Checklist
 

Another checklist, I know. Why? Although this final submission checklist is abbreviated, most professionals, studios, firms, and in-house design and development teams will use and maintain detailed checklists for both manual reviews and checks, as well as automated QA (Quality Assurance) and QC (Quality Control) pipelines. Simply put, checklists before shipping are a means to an end, and can help you catch avoidable errors and issues.

So, this is the last time for this course that you'll need to go through a checklist. Go through every item:

 

Deployed site:

URL loads correctly in an incognito browser window
All pages load without errors
All links work (internal and external)
All interactive features function correctly
Site is usable on mobile, tablet, laptop, desktop (e.g., different viewport sizes and devices and browsers)
No placeholder content remains (e.g., no lorem ipsum text, no "test test test test" text, no temporary images/videos)
Design is consistent across all pages
 

README.md:

Accurately describes the current project (not the P01 version)
Includes what changed from P01 to P02 and why
Lists tools with justification
Contains a working link to the deployed site
 

REFLECTION.md:

Addresses decisions you made
Identifies what worked well
Describes what you'd do differently
Articulates what you learned
Written in an honest, personal voice
 

Repository:

Files are organized and named clearly
No unnecessary files (old drafts, unused assets, etc.)
README.md and REFLECTION.md are in the root directory
Key Takeaways
P02 is your final MVP, aka a complete, polished, documented version of your project
Your P02 should show visible improvement from P01 based on your iteration plan and peer feedback
MVP means everything that matters is done well (not that everything possible is included)
Use the checklist above before submitting; P02 is your final submission for the semester

Overview
Project 02 is the final deliverable of the course. You will ship a complete, polished version of your web project, the Minimum Viable Product you have been building toward since Week 01. 

This project represents the full web development lifecycle: planning, building, deploying, iterating, and reflecting. Your site should show clear evolution from Project 01: not only more content, but better decisions, stronger execution, and a more complete experience.

 

Due: Week 08, Friday, May 8 by 11:59 PM Central Time

Points: 100

Deliverables
 

1. Deployed Web Project
Complete functionality: All planned features work as intended. Nothing is half-built or broken.
Meaningful content: Every page contains real, purposeful content. No placeholders, no filler.
Polished design: Visual design is consistent, intentional, and refined. Typography, color, spacing, and layout work together.
Multi-device usability: The site works well on a range of screens (mobile, tablet, laptop, desktop). Layout adapts, text is readable, and navigation is usable.
Visible improvement from Project 01: The site is meaningfully better than what you submitted for Project 01.
2. README.md
Your GitHub repository must include an updated README.md file with the following five sections:

What is this? A brief description of your project and what it does.
Why does it exist? The purpose behind the project, what problem it solves, or what goal it serves.
What tools did I use? The technologies, frameworks, platforms, and tools you used, and why you chose them.
How to access it: A working link to the deployed site.
What changed from Project 01 to Project 02? A description of what you changed, added, removed, or improved, and why.
3. REFLECTION.md
Your GitHub repository must include a REFLECTION.md file that covers the following four areas:

Decisions: What were the most important decisions you made during this project, and why did you make them?
What worked: What went well? What are you most satisfied with?
What you would do differently: If you started over, what would you change about your approach, tools, or scope?
What you learned: What did you learn about building for the web, about yourself, about your tools, and about the process?
Your reflection should be ~400-800 words. Write in your own voice. Be specific and honest. Avoid generic statements such as "learning a lot"; they do not demonstrate reflection or say much.
