Discussions: Expectations and Grading
Discussions: Expectations and Grading
The Online Discussions component of this course, comprising Discussion Prompts, is an important part of this course.

After you have answered the initial Discussion Prompt with your Initial Post, you need to post at least two (2) substantive responses to your classmates' posts. You are expected to be an active participant in the online discussion forum. If you wait until the last minute to post, you are not allowing for any discussion; you are simply posting your thoughts.

Thus, your initial postings are due by 11:59 PM Central Time on Wednesday, and your replies to classmates are due by 11:59 PM Central Time on Sunday. All discussions must be posted before midnight on the due date.

I expect that you will reference course material in your discussion postings (i.e., readings, videos, coursework, other material).

Requirements for Initial Posts
Reasoned Thought: I expect Initial Posts to reflect some reasoned thought on your part — thought beyond what you might put into a normal email or chat response. Think of them as mini-essays that help you make clear, focused points. These posts should move beyond one simple paragraph. Remember, you're trying to develop your skills, knowledge, and critical thinking; these posts are great practice.

Detailed: Each of these Initial Posts should be at least 150 words, though many will probably need to be 200-300 words. (Note: I am not as interested in the actual word count as I am in the depth of your ideas.)

Semiformal: Your posts should contain some degree of formality: proofread, organized, etc. However, they will also be part of a dialogue, so in that regard, they will differ from an essay you turn in for a class. We will inevitably take some time to reach a mutual understanding of the appropriate level of formality.

Referenced: While you won't always need perfect formal APA citations in your posts, you should look for opportunities to build your argument by referencing our readings, other sources, or your colleagues' comments. Please make a good-faith attempt to use the correct APA citation format.

Civil: There will sometimes be disagreements in our discussion forums; it is important to use those disagreements productively and always remain civil in your engagement with ideas and with one another.

Grading for Initial Posts
I will evaluate your initial posts on a 10-point scale:

If you complete them adequately and on time, you will receive 8s
If you go above and beyond the basic requirements, you will receive 9s
Very good — completed with a great deal of effort and thought — posts will receive 10s.
An initial post will receive a 7 or below if it

is too short
shows little thought
is excessively sloppy in terms of grammar, spelling, or mechanics, especially to the point that it is difficult to understand
lacks civility, like personal attacks
is late
Some initial posts will have slightly different criteria, like your initial introduction forum post. These explanations will be provided in the prompts for those forums.

Note: Initial posts submitted late will receive 0 points.

Requirements for Responses
You ARE NOT expected to reply to every single post — this would be overwhelming. You ARE responsible for reading all the posts in the class, although you can obviously focus your attention on the threads in which you are directly engaged.

For each Discussion, you need to post at least two (2) substantive responses to your classmates' posts.

Grading for Responses
Responses to classmates' posts can be shorter posts than initial posts. I will grade these similarly as secondary posts, but on a 5-point scale.

I will evaluate your responses on a 5-point scale (10 points total):

Adequate posts will receive 4s, and going above and beyond the basic requirements will receive 5s.
Scores of 3 or below will be earned if secondary posts show little thought, are excessively sloppy, or lack civility.
Note: Responses submitted late will receive 0 points.

Additional Posts
You are not limited to the two (2) required responses for each peer: Feel free to post as many shorter, informal comments on the message threads as you like; for instance, a couple of lines to clarify a point or to state your agreement with another author's point of view.

Those of you who are diligent and become active members of these conversations will find that you will receive a high grade for the Discussions component of the course. If you post more than the minimum required posts, doing so is considered going "above and beyond" and will help you to receive a "5" on each post to your peers.

Staying Current
One of your responsibilities in taking an online course is to make it a daily habit to check the Discussion forums and stay current on the conversations taking place there.

Projects: Expectations and Grading
Projects: Expectations and Grading
Projects are large-scale assessments that require you to apply web development skills across the full development lifecycle. They are designed to move you from concept through building, deploying, iterating, and documenting a real web project. There are two major projects in this course, worth a combined 200 points.

The Web Development Lifecycle
Both projects follow a progression that reflects how web projects are built in practice:

Define: Identify what you want to build, who it's for, and why it matters
Build: Use your chosen tools to create a working web project
Deploy: Put your project on the web where others can see and use it
Iterate: Evaluate your work, gather feedback, and make improvements
Document: Explain what you built, the decisions you made, and what you learned
Requirements for Projects
Each project has a unique set of requirements, deliverables, and learning objectives. All project details, including in-depth instructions and resources, are provided on the official assignment page for that project within its corresponding weekly module.

Documentation Requirement
Both projects require documentation in your GitHub repository. Project 01 requires a README. Project 02 requires an updated README and a written reflection. Details are provided in each project's assignment page.

Grading for Projects

Course Description
This course builds on foundational skills in computing applications technologies, focusing on web and software development strategies, prototyping, interactivity, practices, collaboration, and deployment.

CMPA 4303 is about building for the web. You'll spend the term working on a single web project from concept to deployed product — choosing your own tools, making real decisions, and shipping something that works on the open web.

This is not a traditional lecture course. This is a hands-on course where you will learn by doing. Over eight weeks, you will:

Explore what the modern web platform offers and what's possible with today's tools.
Choose your own tools, define your project, and decide what to build and how.
Build a working web project, deploy it to a live URL, and iterate on it based on your own evaluation and peer feedback.
By the end of this course, you will have a deployed web project that showcases your ability to take a concept from idea through building, deployment, iteration, and documentation. You will also have developed confidence in evaluating tools, making decisions, and shipping real work on the web.

Week 01: What the Web Offers
What the Web Offers
You've built websites before. In earlier coursework, you wrote HTML by hand, styled it with CSS, and published it on GitHub Pages. That work taught you the foundations: how pages are structured, how styles are applied, how files connect to each other.

This course builds from there — but the question is different now. Instead of "How do I write a webpage?", the question is: What can the web do, and what will you build with it?

The Web Platform
The web is built on three core technologies that work together:

HTML provides structure. It defines what's on the page — headings, paragraphs, images, links, forms, buttons, sections. You know this already. HTML is the foundation that everything else builds on.

CSS provides presentation. It controls how things look — colors, layout, spacing, typography, animation, responsiveness. You know this too. CSS has continued to grow in capability; things that once required JavaScript or complex workarounds are now achievable with CSS alone.

JavaScript provides behavior. It makes things happen — responding to user actions, updating content without reloading the page, communicating with servers, processing data, creating interactive experiences. This is the piece you haven't worked with directly, and it's the piece that unlocks most of what people think of as "modern" web development.

These three technologies work together in every web browser. They are the platform. Everything you see on the web — from a simple personal site to a complex application — is built on some combination of HTML, CSS, and JavaScript.

What JavaScript Enables
JavaScript is worth understanding not because you need to learn a programming language, but because it enables the things you'll want your projects to do. Here's what becomes possible:

Responding to user actions. A menu that opens when you click a button. A form that validates input as you type. A gallery that cycles through images. An element that appears when you scroll to it.
Dynamic content. Content that changes without reloading the page. A search bar that filters results live. A feed that updates in real time. A counter that increments.
Communicating with external services. Loading data from an API. Displaying weather information, pulling in social media content, connecting to a database. The ability to reach beyond your own files.
Complex interactivity. Drag-and-drop interfaces. Drawing and animation. Audio and video manipulation. Interactive maps. Games.
You don't need to write JavaScript from scratch to make these things happen. Modern tools — AI coding assistants, frameworks, templates, component libraries — can handle the implementation for you. What matters is that you understand what's possible so you can make good decisions about what to build and which tools to use.

Beyond the Basics: What's Available in 2026
The web platform has grown far beyond basic pages. Here are some capabilities that are available to you today:

Responsive design is standard. Websites adapt to phones, tablets, desktops, and everything in between. You've worked with media queries — modern CSS makes this even more flexible with container queries, fluid typography, and layout tools like CSS Grid.

The line between websites and native apps has blurred. Progressive Web Apps (PWAs) can work offline, send notifications, and install on a user's home screen — all from a URL, no app store required.

APIs and integrations connect your project to the rest of the web. You can embed maps, pull in data, integrate with payment systems, connect to AI services, and use third-party tools without building everything from scratch.

Getting a site live used to require servers, hosting configuration, and technical infrastructure. Today, platforms like GitHub Pages, Netlify, Vercel, and Cloudflare Pages make deployment as simple as pushing a commit or dragging a folder.

AI coding assistants like Cursor, GitHub Copilot, and Claude can help you write code, debug problems, generate content, and work through implementation challenges. They're tools — useful when used thoughtfully, misleading when used carelessly.

What This Means for Your Project
Throughout this course, you'll build a single web project that evolves across the semester. You'll start with a concept, choose your tools, build a working prototype, add interactivity, deploy it, get feedback, iterate, and ship a final version.

The web gives you the platform. The tools give you leverage. The decisions are yours.

This week, your job is to explore — look at what people are building on the web, get a sense of what tools are out there, and start thinking about what you want to make.

Key Takeaways
The web platform is HTML (structure) + CSS (presentation) + JavaScript (behavior)
JavaScript enables interactivity, dynamic content, and external connections — you don't need to master it to use it
Modern web capabilities include responsive design, PWAs, APIs, easy deployment, and AI-assisted development
Your project this semester will use some combination of these capabilities, depending on what you want to build

Week 01: The Modern Tool Landscape
The Modern Tool Landscape
In earlier coursework, you used a specific set of tools: VS Code for editing, GitHub Desktop for version control, and GitHub Pages for deployment. Those tools were chosen for you — the course told you what to use and how to use it.

This course is different. You'll choose your own tools based on what your project needs and what works for you. That means understanding what's out there.

This page isn't a recommendation list. It's a map of the landscape. The goal is awareness, not prescription. Next week, you'll evaluate specific tools and make your own choices.

Categories of Tools
Modern web development involves several categories of tools. You don't need all of them — what you use depends on what you're building.

Code Editors and Development Environments
A code editor is where you write and organize your project files. You've used VS Code, which remains one of the most popular editors. But there are other options:

VS Code — The editor you know. Extensible, well-documented, widely used. Free.
Cursor — A code editor built around AI assistance. Based on VS Code but designed for AI-first development. Helps you write, edit, and understand code through conversation.
Web-based editors — Tools like GitHub Codespaces, Replit, and StackBlitz let you develop entirely in the browser, with nothing to install locally.
The differences between editors are real but not dramatic. What matters more than which editor you use is how effectively you use it.

AI Coding Assistants
AI tools have become part of everyday web development. They can help you write code, explain errors, generate content, and solve implementation problems. Some are built into editors; others are standalone:

Editor-integrated assistants — Cursor, GitHub Copilot, and similar tools work directly in your code editor. They suggest code as you type, answer questions about your project, and help you debug.
Conversational assistants — Tools like Claude, ChatGPT, and Gemini let you describe what you want in plain language and get code, explanations, and guidance in return.
Specialized tools — Some tools generate specific things: UI components, color schemes, images, placeholder content, deployment configurations.
AI assistants are not magic. They work best when you can describe what you want clearly, evaluate whether the result is correct, and know enough about your project to guide the conversation. They're tools — like any tool, their value depends on how you use them.

Frameworks and Libraries
A framework provides pre-built structure and functionality so you don't start from zero. A library provides specific tools you can use within your own structure. Some popular options:

Static site generators — Tools like Astro, Eleventy, and Hugo take your content and produce a fast, static website. Good for portfolios, blogs, documentation sites.
JavaScript frameworks — React, Vue, Svelte, and others provide structure for building interactive interfaces. They're powerful but add complexity. Whether you need one depends on your project.
CSS frameworks — Tailwind CSS, Bootstrap, and similar tools provide pre-built styling systems. They can accelerate development if you're comfortable with their approach.
Component libraries — Pre-built UI elements (buttons, forms, navigation, cards) that you can use directly in your project. Useful for moving quickly.
Not every project needs a framework. Some projects are better served by plain HTML, CSS, and a bit of JavaScript. The right choice depends on what you're building and what you're comfortable with.

Version Control and Collaboration
You've used GitHub Desktop to push files to GitHub. Version control has more to offer:

Git — The underlying system that tracks changes to your files. GitHub Desktop, the command line, and editor integrations all use Git.
GitHub — The platform where your project lives online. Beyond hosting files, GitHub provides deployment (GitHub Pages), collaboration tools, issue tracking, and project management.
Other Git interfaces — The command line, VS Code's built-in Git support, and other tools all connect to the same system. Use whatever feels natural to you.
This course requires GitHub for project hosting. How you interact with it — Desktop app, command line, editor integration — is your choice.

Hosting and Deployment
Getting your project online is easier than it has ever been:

GitHub Pages — You've used this. Free hosting directly from your GitHub repository. Good for static sites.
Netlify — Free tier available. Deploys from a Git repository with automatic builds. Supports forms, serverless functions, and custom domains.
Vercel — Built by the team behind Next.js. Similar to Netlify. Good for framework-based projects.
Cloudflare Pages — Fast, free hosting with a global content delivery network.
All of these platforms can deploy a project from a GitHub repository. The process is similar: connect your repo, configure your build settings (if any), and your site is live with a URL.

How to Think About Tool Choices
You don't need to decide now. Next week, you'll evaluate tools and make choices for your project. For now, keep these ideas in mind:

Start with what you're building. The project drives the tool selection, not the other way around. A portfolio site has different needs than an interactive web app.

If plain HTML and CSS can do what you need, that's a valid choice. Simpler is better until it isn't — add complexity only when it solves a real problem.

Your comfort matters too. If a tool confuses you more than it helps, it's the wrong tool for you right now. Effectiveness comes from understanding, not from using the most popular option.

Whatever tools you use this semester, they'll be different in five years. The ability to evaluate, choose, and learn new tools is more durable than mastering any specific one.

Key Takeaways
Modern web development has several categories of tools: editors, AI assistants, frameworks, version control, and hosting platforms
You don't need all of them — what you use depends on what you're building
AI coding assistants are part of the picture, useful when used thoughtfully
GitHub is required for this course; everything else is your choice
Start with what you're building, not with which tools are popular
